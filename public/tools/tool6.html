How to fill this in correctly
Enter every item that could be running at the same time during your busiest period — this is your peak demand scenario.

The flow rate for each item comes from its datasheet or nameplate — look for "air consumption" in l/min or CFM.

The duty cycle is how much of the time that item is actually consuming air. A cylinder that strokes once every 10 seconds has roughly 20% duty. A continuous blow-off nozzle is 100%.

Where will the compressor be installed?
Site conditions directly affect how much air your compressor can deliver. A compressor at altitude or high temperature delivers significantly less air than its nameplate suggests.
⚠
Why this matters more than most people realise
Compressor datasheets are rated at sea level, 20°C and 0% humidity. If your site is at 500m altitude and 38°C ambient, the same compressor delivers approximately 12–18% less air than the nameplate states. This is why compressors sized without site correction fail to maintain pressure on hot summer days.

What pressure does your equipment need?
The compressor must deliver more pressure than your equipment needs — the difference is lost to pipeline friction, filters, dryers, and fittings.
💡
Pressure at the tool vs pressure at the compressor
Your equipment has a minimum operating pressure — that is what it needs at the point of use, at the end of your pipework. The compressor must be set higher to compensate for losses through filters, dryers, hoses and fittings. A typical well-maintained system loses 0.5–1.0 bar between compressor and point of use.
Typical: 6–7 bar g for most pneumatic equipment

These allowances ensure your compressor is correctly sized for real-world conditions — not just the theoretical peak demand.

Why allowances matter
Every compressed air system leaks — typically 10–25% of total flow in an average industrial system. You also want headroom for future equipment. These allowances are not padding — they are engineering realism.
